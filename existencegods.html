Existence of Gods and Unifying Theories

To understand this discussion, you must first understand the concepts behind The Golden Dialetik. It is recommended to review the following web pages before tackling the concept of the existence of Gods: Preamble to the Existence of God and the Existence of God. (Note the singular as opposed to plural)

Under the assumption that a single God exists, then the next step is to question if multiple Gods could also exist, such as the Gods of Greece or the Hindu Gods. The answer likewise is in the positive, but then why do some peoples see one God and others see many?

Each culture and/or person in these cultures, mirrors back the same source, the Golden Dialetik, which for some cultures is a kaleidoscope of faces and images. Although different from the singular “God,” they all constitute unique variations in regards to this ultimate source, from which that culture can create their own unifying theory of the universe; the underlying goal for everyone is to have a cohesive picture of reality.

The Golden Dialetik is the cosmic kaleidoscope that could be likened to a golden mirrored disco ball. People try to translate the massive amount of “faces” turned towards them, and from these impressions arise unifying theories. There are three basic premises to unifying theories:

• Unifying theories of the universe try to explain everything,
• The theory behind a unifying theory of the universe is that everything is connected
• Each “theory” was accomplished within the limitations of the technology of the society at the time.

The use of the word technology above includes both social and physical sciences.

As an infant explores a world, a new world for them, they need to touch and taste everything. People with very young children are familiar with toddlers grabbing things and putting them in their mouths. This is their way to learn these objects, but also to learn what the surface of these objects are like, building up an inventory of cognitive impressions with which to create a more consistent interpretation of the world about them.

This collecting of cognitive impressions spans through all the senses, but at a certain point to enter the communicable world, these objects need names. Names are the adult’s way of ordering their inventory of cognitive sensations, but naming things leaves the last step of bringing an understandable order to the perception of the universe. This is where unifying theories step in.

In a first pass, the One, such as the Judeo-Christian-Abrahamic God, contains all the faces of the Gods in another culture:

When a culture perceives a plurality of Gods, such as the cultures of ancient Greece and the Hindu religion of India, they perceive the same source, but through a variety of different dialetikal facets. In India, the Hindu Trimurti consists of Brahma the Creator, Vishnu the Preserver and Siva the Destroyer. Each of the major traits of these three Hindu God’s can be attributed to the Judeo-Christian-Muslim God. The biblical God is the Creator, and at times God is the Destroyer (Sodom and Gomorrah or the promised climactic battle at Armageddon), but in addition God is the Preserver, nurturing humankind (Adam and Eve in the Garden of Eden). As Western culture perceives a single reflection of God, the Hindu religion perceives three different faces, not one.

All the attributes of all the Gods can be found in the One. Conversely, all the qualities in the One can be viewed separately. Each choice has its advantages and disadvantages, but from the perspective of Crystal Choices, the view of the Golden Dialetik is the same, whether of a single God or multiple Gods.
